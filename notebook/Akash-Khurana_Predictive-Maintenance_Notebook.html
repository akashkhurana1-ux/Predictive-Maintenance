Business Objective


This project aims to develop a machine learning model to predict engine failures using sensor data. Early prediction enables proactive maintenance, reducing downtime, repair costs, and safety risks.

Key Benefits


- Reduce unplanned breakdowns
- Optimize maintenance schedules
- Improve operational efficiency

This project can be utilized by Automobile manufacturers, fleet management companies, and maintenance service providers as a data-driven solutions to enhance engine reliability and optimize maintenance planning. By continuously monitoring key engine health indicators such as RPM, oil pressure, fuel pressure, coolant pressure, and temperature readings, machine learning models can identify patterns associated with engine degradation or potential failures. This enables timely maintenance interventions, reducing the risk of unexpected breakdowns and improving operational efficiency.

The dataset used in this project contains sensor readings that represent the operating characteristics of both small and large engines commonly found in vehicles, portable generators, lawn equipment, and other compact machinery. Since engines vary in their operating ranges depending on their size and application, the dataset captures a broad range of RPM, pressure, and temperature values. This makes it suitable for developing predictive maintenance models applicable across diverse engine types and operating conditions.
 

Objective



The objective of this project is to develop a machine learning–based predictive maintenance model capable of analyzing historical engine sensor data to detect potential failure conditions. The model will classify whether an engine is operating under normal conditions or requires maintenance, based on its sensor readings.

The implementation of this predictive maintenance solution is expected to deliver the following benefits:

- Reduce unexpected engine failures and associated repair costs
- Improve overall engine reliability, performance, and operational lifespan
- Enable proactive and optimized maintenance scheduling to minimize operational downtime
- Provide actionable, data-driven insights to manufacturers, fleet operators, and service providers to support informed maintenance and operational decisions
 

Data Description



* Engine_RPM: The number of revolutions per minute (RPM) of the engine, indicating engine speed. It is defined in Revolutions per Minute (RPM).
* Lub_Oil_Pressure: The pressure of the lubricating oil in the engine, essential for reducing friction and wear. It is defined in bar or kilopascals (kPa)
* Fuel_Pressure: The pressure at which fuel is supplied to the engine, critical for proper combustion. It is defined in bar or kilopascals (kPa)
* Coolant_Pressure: The pressure of the engine coolant, affecting engine temperature regulation. It is defined in bar or kilopascals (kPa)
* Lub_Oil_Temperature: The temperature of the lubricating oil, which impacts viscosity and engine performance. It is defined in degrees Celsius (°C)
* Coolant_Temperature: The temperature of the engine coolant, crucial for preventing overheating. It is defined in degrees Celsius (°C)
* Engine_Condition: A categorical or numerical label representing the health of the engine, potentially indicating normal operation or various levels of wear and failure risks. It is defined as a categorical variable (0/1) representing a state such as "0 = Off/False/Active" and "1 = On/True/Faulty"

#### Observations & Comments

- Engine RPM
- Most engine operations are concentrated between 500 - 1000 RPM. There are a few cases of high-RPM, which could be the engines in stress conditions.
- This High RPM indicates engine under heavy load, which can cause wear,overheating & mechanical issues.

- Lube Oil Pressure
- Lubrication pressure is mostly stable in the 2 - 5 range. This suggests the range of 2-3 is the normal operating conditions. There are very few extreme values (0-1 & 6).
- Very Low Pressure could mean insufficient lubrication, which is definitely concerning leaing to friction, overheating & thus, engine damage.
- High lube oil pressure can damage seals or components.

- Fuel Pressure
- Fuel pressure is centered around 5 - 10 units. There're some cases of low & high pressure instances.
- Low Fuel Pressure could mean fuel is not properly getting delivered to engine, which can causes engine to not produce enough power.
- On the other hand, High Fuel Pressure means over-fueling leading to fuel wastage & emissions issues.
- This parameter seems to have directly impact on engine performance.

- Coolant Pressure
- Coolant pressure is mostly concentrated between 1 - 3. There are few anomalies on right side (higher pressure).
- Higher pressure could cause burst in collant compartment/hoses or damage to radiator.

- Lube Oil Temperature
- Lub oil temperature is distributed around 75 - 80°C with minimal fluctuation.
- There are cases of higher temperature, which will cause Lube-Oil thinning, leading to reduced lubrication & hence faster engine wear.

- Coolant Temperature
- Coolant temperature is mostly within 70 - 90°C. very few extreme overheating cases.
- However, high cooland temperature means engine might be overheating, causing engine failure (direct failure trigger).
- This could be 1 of the strongest failure indicators.

- Engine Condition
- This is the Target Variable.
- The data clearly shows more engines in “faulty” (1) condition than normal (0).
- It means more engines are showing abnormal sensor patterns & fewer engines are in safe/stable operating range.
- It indicates combined effect of all above parameters, since for individual engine parameters largely operate within stable ranges.

EDA Conclusion :



#### Feature Importance :

Based on the exploratory data analysis, the following features are expected to be the most predictive for identifying engine failure (Engine_Condition):

- Engine_RPM (Primary Driver): The multivariate and bivariate analyses consistently show that failures are highly correlated with specific RPM ranges. Normal operations show a stable distribution, whereas failures often occur at the extremes of the RPM spectrum, making this a critical feature for the tree-based models (RF/XGBoost).

- Lub_Oil_Pressure & Coolant_Pressure: These sensors exhibit the most significant variance when the engine state transitions to "Faulty." The boxplot analysis indicates that lower-than-normal lubrication pressure is a strong precursor to mechanical stress and failure.

- Coolant_Temperature: The distribution plots reveal a clear upward shift in temperature preceding a failure. This "thermal signature" is a classic indicator of engine overheating and is expected to have high feature importance.

- Feature Interaction (Pressure vs. Temperature): The correlation heatmap suggests that the interaction between Fuel_Pressure and Coolant_Temperature provides a multi-dimensional signal that simple linear models might miss.

#### Inputs to model selection :

Because the relationships between these features and the target variable are non-linear, XGBoost and Random Forest are the ideal choices to capture these complex interactions and maximize Recall for proactive maintenance.

Hence, it would require the use of ensemble models such as Random Forest & XGBoost, which can effectively capture such interactions.

# Reusable Evaluation Function
def evaluate_model(model, X_test, y_test, model_name):
    preds = model.predict(X_test)
    probs = model.predict_proba(X_test)[:, 1]

    tn, fp, fn, tp = confusion_matrix(y_test, preds).ravel()

    return {
        "Model": model_name,
        "FP": fp,
        "FN": fn,
        "Accuracy": accuracy_score(y_test, preds),
        "Precision": precision_score(y_test, preds),
        "Recall": recall_score(y_test, preds),
        "ROC_AUC": roc_auc_score(y_test, probs)
    }
